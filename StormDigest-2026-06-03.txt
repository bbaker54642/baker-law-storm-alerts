STORM: EF-1 Tornado — Nacogdoches County, TX (May 30, 2026)
---
An EF-1 tornado moved through Nacogdoches County on May 30. [1] The National Weather Service confirmed peak winds of 100 mph. [1] Nine homes in the county sustained major damage. Twenty-five more had some level of damage. [2] The county judge issued a local disaster declaration the following day. [2]

The insurance phase has started. I handle claims that come out of storms like this. What I see consistently is that carriers look for ways to reduce the amount they pay.

Tornado claims face a specific tactic. Carriers argue that damaged roofs were already deteriorating before the storm. They claim tree falls on structures were not storm-caused but the result of pre-existing weakness. They invoke exclusions for settling or earth movement to reclassify direct wind damage into excluded categories. The goal in each case is the same: pay less than the policy actually requires.

The winds on May 30 were documented and confirmed by the National Weather Service at 100 mph. [1] That documentation matters. Direct wind damage is a covered peril under virtually every Texas homeowners policy. When a carrier reclassifies that damage into an excluded category, it is making a deliberate choice to shift costs onto people.

A local disaster declaration does not change how a carrier handles a claim. People in Nacogdoches County need to know those two things are entirely separate.

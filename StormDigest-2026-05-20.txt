STORM: Severe Thunderstorms - Central Texas (Williamson County and surrounding counties), May 19-20, 2026
---
Severe thunderstorms hit Williamson County and Central Texas on the night of May 19, bringing wind gusts reported at up to 80 mph and quarter-size hail. [1] The National Weather Service issued destructive storm warnings for Jarrell and surrounding communities, stating mobile homes would be heavily damaged and considerable damage to roofs, windows, and vehicles was expected. [1] Power outages affected more than 6,400 customers in Williamson County alone. [2]

I see this same pattern after every storm like this. People file claims. Carriers slow-walk the process.

They lead with causation disputes. Adjusters document pre-existing wear, age-of-roof conditions, and prior damage to reduce what they owe. A short inspection produces a report that becomes the company's permanent position on your loss.

Texas separates actual cash value from replacement cost value coverage. Under ACV, carriers deduct depreciation before paying. Under RCV, you recover depreciation only after repairs are complete and documented. Most people find out which policy they have after the first check arrives short.

Texas law sets hard deadlines. Carriers must acknowledge a claim within 15 days and accept or deny it within 15 business days of receiving required documentation. Missing those deadlines triggers penalty interest under the Texas Insurance Code.

A denial is not a final word. It is a position, and positions based on inapplicable exclusions can be challenged.

STORM: Severe Storms / Tornadic Activity - Victoria County and Calhoun County (Coastal Texas), April 24 - May 1, 2026
---
Severe storms with tornadic activity hit Victoria County and coastal Calhoun County from April 24 through May 1, with straight-line winds reported between 70 and 120 miles per hour. [1] Authorities documented 133 damaged structures in Victoria County, with 44 receiving major damage or completely destroyed. [2] Governor Abbott added both counties to Texas' severe weather disaster declaration, and the SBA opened a Disaster Loan Outreach Center in Victoria on May 18. [3] [4]

I have handled coastal storm claims long enough to know how this plays out.

Carriers working TWIA policies use the wind-versus-flood distinction aggressively. They attribute damage to storm surge or flooding when wind drove the water. That is a direct challenge to your coverage, and it turns on what the evidence shows about sequence of damage.

TWIA has its own claims process, its own appraisal clause, and its own notice deadlines. Policyholders who miss TWIA's windows lose protections they cannot recover. Most people do not know those deadlines exist until it is too late.

SBA disaster loans are available in these counties now. They are loans. Homeowners who accept SBA funds before their insurance claim is resolved can reduce their leverage with the carrier.

133 damaged structures in one county is not a small event. It is 133 separate negotiations with insurers who have every incentive to pay less than the damage warrants. Whether people recover what they are owed depends on how those negotiations go.

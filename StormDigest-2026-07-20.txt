STORM: Texas Hill Country / South Central Texas flooding — federal FEMA declaration update, July 11–17, 2026
---
A federal major disaster declaration for the July 2026 Texas flooding was approved on July 19, covering 28 counties including Uvalde, Kerr, and Kendall. [1] Two people died in the flooding. [2] More than 270 state rescues were conducted, and a farm-to-market bridge in Uvalde County partially collapsed. [3]

A federal declaration means affected residents may register for FEMA Individual Assistance at DisasterAssistance.gov. That program provides temporary housing funds and limited repair assistance. It is not a substitute for an insurance claim. The two can run at the same time.

Carriers in disaster-declared counties use that declaration to their advantage. When a loss involves both wind and flood, carriers categorize as much as possible as flood. Flood damage is either uninsured or falls under a separate flood policy. Carriers know federal assistance exists, and they use that knowledge when deciding how to write their estimates.

I have seen this pattern after every declared disaster in South and Central Texas. Wind damage that preceded flooding is still a covered wind claim under a homeowners policy. The sequence of events matters. The policy in force on the date of loss controls what the carrier owes.

FEMA Individual Assistance has a cap. Insurance does not carry the same cap. Both processes belong on the table for any policyholder dealing with damage from this storm.

STORM: EF-1 tornado — northwest San Antonio (Bexar County), July 15, 2026
---
People across the northwest side of San Antonio are dealing with property damage after an EF-1 tornado touched down near Shavano Park on July 15, 2026 and traveled four miles across the area. [1] The National Weather Service confirmed peak winds of 100 miles per hour. [1] A large section of the roof at the Oasis Apartments on UTSA Boulevard was torn from the building, affecting three fourth-floor units and displacing about ten to twelve students. [2] The Palladium IMAX theater and commercial properties near The Rim also sustained damage. [2]

The property owner's insurance covers the apartment structure. The dispute will be about scope. Carriers send adjusters who measure what they saw on a single visit, write an estimate that covers some of the damaged materials, and close the claim. Owners then discover the estimate does not cover full repair costs.

People living in the damaged units carry the separate question of their personal property. A landlord's commercial property policy does not cover tenants' clothing, furniture, or electronics. Renters insurance exists for that purpose. People without it have no claim to file for what they lost inside the unit.

I have worked wind damage claims in San Antonio for years. A 100 mph EF-1 tornado that tears a roof section off a four-story building is not an ambiguous loss. The cause of loss is wind. The dispute that follows is about how much the carrier will pay to restore it.

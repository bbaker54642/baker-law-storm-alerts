STORM: Severe Thunderstorm Complex with Flash Flooding - Corpus Christi / Coastal Bend (Nueces County and surrounding coastal counties), May 25-28, 2026
---
Homeowners and business owners across the Corpus Christi area are now in the claims process after a series of severe thunderstorms and flooding swept through Nueces County and the surrounding Coastal Bend from late May into May 28. [1]

The storms knocked out power to more than 29,000 AEP Texas customers, with the Corpus Christi district hit hardest. [2] A commercial structure collapsed onto the roof of a Stripes gas station in Corpus Christi. Strong winds tore bricks and wiring from the wall of an apartment building in Flour Bluff. [1] Flood warnings were issued for Aransas, Calhoun, Nueces, Refugio, and San Patricio counties as Oso Creek rose above flood stage. [3] Debris cleanup is still underway in neighborhoods near Oso Bay. [4]

Here is what typically happens next.

Insurance companies send adjusters quickly. But fast does not mean thorough. What they document on the first visit controls the claim. Adjusters often miss wind-driven water intrusion, secondary structural damage, and damage behind siding or beneath rooflines.

In TWIA territory along the Coastal Bend, carriers regularly invoke the separate wind/hail deductible. That number often runs 1 to 3 percent of the dwelling value before any coverage applies. Carriers also try to assign as much loss as possible to flooding, where NFIP limits cap payouts. That separation is where policies get turned against policyholders.

Depreciation disputes follow. Materials cost more now than when your policy was written. That gap comes out of your settlement check.

The policy you paid for covers what the storm actually did. What you receive and what you are owed are frequently two different figures.

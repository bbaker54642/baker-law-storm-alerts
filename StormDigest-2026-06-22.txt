STORM: Tropical Storm Arthur - Matagorda, Galveston, and Brazoria Counties, Texas
---
Tropical Storm Arthur made landfall near Matagorda County on June 17 as the first named
tropical storm of the 2026 Atlantic hurricane season. [1] Peak sustained winds reached
45 mph, with gusts of 64 mph recorded at a Galveston monitoring station. [2] More than
8 inches of rain fell on parts of Brazoria County, including Freeport and Clute. [2]
Storm surge exceeded 2 feet across parts of Galveston Bay. [2] AccuWeather estimates
total damage and economic loss from Arthur at $4 to $6 billion. [3]

People along the Texas coast are now starting the claims process. The first disagreement
is almost always about the line between wind and water. TWIA covers wind. A separate
flood policy covers water. The adjuster drawing that line works for the carrier.

I have seen TWIA adjusters routinely undervalue coastal storm damage. Roof estimates
come in below what licensed contractors actually charge to do the work. Storm surge
damage gets pushed to the flood policy because water entered at the ground rather than
from above. Wind-driven rain through a failed window or door can go either direction
depending on who is writing the estimate.

TWIA policies contain an appraisal process that policyholders can use to dispute the
carrier's valuation. That process has a deadline. The deadline does not extend because
the adjuster was slow.

STORM: June 14-15 Severe Weather - Hail, Flooding, and Wind - Aransas, Nueces,
  Calhoun, and Coastal Texas
---
Large hail, damaging wind gusts, and flash flooding swept through coastal Texas beginning
June 14. [1] Governor Abbott issued a state disaster proclamation for 101 Texas counties
on June 15, specifically including Aransas, Nueces, Calhoun, Matagorda, Refugio, and
Galveston counties. [1] [2] Damage estimates are still being assessed.

After a multi-county hail event, carriers assign high claim volumes to adjusters working
quickly. Fast inspections produce incomplete estimates. I see it consistently: hail
strikes on metal flashing, gutters, HVAC equipment, and fascia get documented as no
damage because the adjuster did not look long enough.

On older roofs, carriers apply depreciation. Actual cash value payments reflect the
carrier's estimate of what the roof was worth before the storm, not what it costs to
repair now. That number is lower than replacement cost, and the policy allows the carrier
to pay the lower number first.

Homeowners in Aransas, Nueces, and Calhoun counties should photograph every room showing
ceiling discoloration or cracked drywall before removing anything. The interior evidence
of a hail storm disappears quickly. The claim does not reopen when it is gone.

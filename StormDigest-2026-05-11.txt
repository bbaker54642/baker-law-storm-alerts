STORM: Severe Thunderstorm / Downburst - Calhoun County (Point Comfort), TX - May 1, 2026
---
People in Calhoun County are beginning the insurance process right now. The downburst that struck Point Comfort on May 1 produced a recorded wind gust of 119 miles per hour. It toppled high-voltage transmission towers. It flipped semi-trucks. It reduced metal structures to twisted wreckage. Calhoun County and the City of Point Comfort declared local disasters the same weekend. Governor Abbott added the county to the state disaster declaration on May 7.

Coastal Texas policies often carry wind and hail deductibles calculated as a percentage of the dwelling's insured value, not a flat dollar figure. On a home insured for $300,000, a 2% wind deductible is $6,000 out of pocket before coverage begins. Carriers sometimes apply these percentage deductibles to straight-line wind events even when no hurricane or named storm was involved.

Carriers also commission their own engineering and damage reports. Those reports are designed to protect the carrier's financial position. They frequently attribute damage to pre-existing conditions, deferred maintenance, or original construction rather than the storm event. In Calhoun County right now, that process is already underway on hundreds of damaged properties.

A state disaster declaration opens access to state recovery programs. It does not alter how a private carrier is required to handle your claim. Every policy is a separate contract, and carriers interpret that contract in their own interest first.

People who receive low estimates or denials have the right to request the full claim file and to challenge the findings.

STORM: Severe Thunderstorm / Tornadic Activity - Victoria County (Crossroads Region), TX - Late April-Early May 2026
---
People in Victoria County are filing claims right now. Tornadic storms and powerful straight-line winds swept through the Crossroads region in late April and early May, leaving widespread structural damage, downed power lines and destroyed property across multiple communities. Governor Abbott added Victoria County to the state disaster declaration on May 7 and directed state and SBA officials to begin preliminary damage assessments.

Victoria County sits in the Texas Windstorm Insurance Association coverage zone. That matters. Homeowners with TWIA windstorm policies face a claims process that is separate from their homeowners policy and runs on its own rules. TWIA has specific dispute deadlines. If a policyholder disagrees with a TWIA adjustment, the window to demand appraisal or file a formal complaint is not open-ended.

After tornadic events, carriers often dispute whether damage was caused by rotating wind, straight-line wind, or rain intrusion. Each classification can trigger a different deductible or a different coverage section. The classification is made by the carrier's adjuster first, and it strongly influences the final estimate.

I work in this region. After storms like these, I see the same sequence repeat. Adjusters arrive with their estimates. Policyholders sign off because they don't know they can push back. Checks arrive that don't reflect the full cost of repair.

Texas law gives policyholders specific rights after a covered storm loss. Those rights don't disappear when a carrier sends a check.

STORM: Severe Thunderstorm / Hail / Flash Flooding - Aransas, Nueces & San Patricio Counties (Corpus Christi, Rockport, Port Aransas) - May 10, 2026
---
People across Aransas, Nueces and San Patricio counties are assessing damage this morning. The severe storm system that moved through the Coastal Bend on May 10 produced wind gusts between 60 and 80 miles per hour in Corpus Christi, Rockport, Port Aransas and Portland. The National Weather Service issued flash flood warnings for Aransas and San Patricio counties, with 2.5 to 3.5 inches of rain falling in a short window. A Severe Thunderstorm Watch covered 15 South Texas counties through the overnight hours.

Claims are being filed right now.

Policies on the Texas coast are intentionally layered. Many homeowners carry a separate TWIA windstorm policy and a private homeowners policy and possibly a flood policy. When storm wind and flooding both contribute to damage, carriers argue about who is responsible. The wind carrier characterizes damage as flood-related. The flood carrier characterizes it as wind-driven water intrusion. The policyholder sits in the middle waiting.

That dispute pattern is not incidental. It is a documented method carriers use to reduce payouts after coastal events. The argument can take months to resolve while a damaged property continues to deteriorate.

Damage in Aransas County in particular is often caught between two or three separate policies with two or three separate adjusters producing competing conclusions.

The notes, photos and written documentation you produce in the first 72 hours become part of the claim file. They matter in disputes that can stretch six months or more.

STORM: EF3 Tornado Outbreak - Parker County (Mineral Wells) and North Texas - April 24-28, 2026
---
People in Mineral Wells and Parker County are in the early stages of a long recovery. The EF3 tornado that struck on April 28 packed winds measured at 145 miles per hour. It destroyed two major industrial facilities and damaged residential areas across a two-to-three mile corridor. Governor Abbott declared a state disaster for Parker, Wise, Lamar and Palo Pinto counties. The proclamation was renewed in May.

When a tornado produces EF3 damage, the insurance dispute often centers on scope. Carriers send adjusters who document visible damage. What they frequently miss: compromised structural connections, out-of-plumb walls, damaged framing behind intact surfaces, foundation movement. A carrier that pays only for what was visually apparent on the first walk-through is almost always undervaluing the claim.

EF3 tornadoes stress every load-bearing point in a structure. Roofs that stayed in place may be lifting at edges. Walls still standing may no longer meet code. These conditions require structural assessment, not a standard field adjustment.

After large tornado events, carriers sometimes close claims quickly by issuing what they describe as full payment, sometimes with a release of claim buried in the paperwork. Signing and cashing that check can end the claim regardless of what further damage appears later.

Parker County homeowners are entitled to a written explanation of every covered item and every exclusion applied. That document is the starting point for any dispute.

People rebuild with what the carrier pays. They rarely find out they were underpaid until the repairs fail.

--- DRAFT POSTS ---

STORM: Severe High Winds / Coastal Storm - Calhoun and Victoria Counties
---
People in Calhoun and Victoria counties are now living inside a state disaster declaration. That declaration opens recovery resources. It does not open your insurance company's checkbook.

High-wind events along the Texas coast produce a predictable pattern of claim handling. Adjusters arrive and sort damage into categories. Wind. Pre-existing deterioration. Wear and tear. Maintenance failure. Each category that gets relabeled as something other than storm damage is money the carrier keeps.

Point Comfort took a direct hit. Homes and businesses across Calhoun County lost structures, roofing, and power infrastructure. When adjusters inspect those properties, the pressure is to attribute as much damage as possible to deferred maintenance. Carriers use that framing specifically because maintenance failures are excluded from most policies.

Calhoun County sits in TWIA territory. If your windstorm coverage runs through TWIA, your claim enters a separate process with separate adjusters and a separate timeline. TWIA has faced repeated litigation over how it handles coastal storm claims. That history is public record.

What policyholders are entitled to is what the policy language says they are entitled to. That means you have the right to request a full copy of your policy, a written explanation of every denial, and an itemized estimate of what the carrier is paying and why.

A state disaster declaration does not accelerate claim resolution. Policyholders have to work that process on their own.

STORM: Supercell / Baseball-Sized Hail - Texas Hill Country (Mason, Llano, Gillespie Counties)
---
People across Mason, Llano, Blanco, Burnet, and Gillespie counties woke up on May 6 to baseball-sized hail. Roofs took the first hit. The insurance process is the second one.

What carriers do after a large-scale hail event is consistent. They send adjusters in volume. Volume means speed, and speed means missed damage. A hail claim closed in 48 hours almost never reflects a full inspection of the property.

Hill Country construction is not uniform. Older homes, metal roofing, stone structures, and mixed materials each respond to hail differently. Adjusters trained primarily on composition shingle roofs in suburban North Texas are not equipped to properly assess damage on a 1950s stone-and-metal structure in Llano County. Carriers send who they have available.

The depreciation dispute comes next. Carriers apply actual cash value calculations that reduce payout on older roofs even when those roofs were fully functional before May 6. Replacement cost coverage is designed to address that. What policyholders actually receive depends entirely on how the adjuster documented the pre-storm condition of the roof and what depreciation figures the carrier applies.

Hail damage missed in the initial inspection does not automatically reopen a claim. Texas law limits that window and places the burden on the policyholder to act.

STORM: Hailstorm - Katy / Cinco Ranch / West Houston (Fort Bend and Harris Counties)
---
People in Katy, Cinco Ranch, and the neighborhoods around them are now dealing with hail damage across a large section of west Houston. The roofs took the worst of it. The insurance fights are starting.

What happens after a widespread suburban hail event is predictable. Contractors move fast to document damage. Carrier adjusters move to counter their estimates. The gap between what a roofing contractor documents in the field and what an adjuster approves on a form is where most of the money gets lost.

Adjusters working a large event are under internal pressure to close claims. That pressure is not visible to the homeowner, but the results are. Estimates come back that minimize scope, miss damage to gutters and siding and skylights, and apply depreciation in ways that reduce payment significantly below actual repair costs.

Fort Bend and Harris County homeowners have been through this before. After 2017. After 2019. After every significant hail season on record. The underpayment pattern is not coincidental. It is the predictable output of a claims system built to manage losses.

What policyholders are entitled to under a replacement cost policy is the full cost of restoring the property to its pre-storm condition. Not an estimate calibrated to a budget. Not a cash value figure that penalizes a roof for its age without accounting for its condition before the storm hit.

The difference between a carrier's first offer and the actual repair cost is often several thousand dollars per property.

STORM: Severe Thunderstorm / Flash Flooding - Corpus Christi, Rockport, Aransas Pass, Fulton (Aransas, Nueces, San Patricio Counties)
---
People in Rockport, Corpus Christi, Aransas Pass, and Fulton are dealing with flooding and storm damage right now. The water is still moving in some areas.

This stretch of the Texas coast does not get gentle claims handling. Carriers that write policies here know these storms come. They price the risk accordingly. They also build in every available exclusion to limit what they pay when the storm arrives.

Flooding is the word adjusters will use first and use often. If a loss gets labeled a flood, a standard homeowner's policy typically does not cover it. Flood has a specific legal meaning in insurance, and it is a definition that benefits the carrier. Wind-driven rain, drainage overflow, storm surge - carriers push as much water damage as possible into the flood category regardless of how the water entered the structure.

Aransas County and San Patricio County are under flash flood warnings. If your property has water damage, the first thing your adjuster will determine is what kind of water caused it. That single determination shapes every payment decision that follows.

TWIA-covered properties face the same split. Flood damage routes to the National Flood Insurance Program. Wind damage routes to TWIA. When both caused damage, each carrier argues the other one owes the larger share.

Policyholders caught between those two systems carry most of the burden of proving what caused what.

STORM: Mother's Day Severe Thunderstorm / Large Hail / Tornado Outbreak - Statewide Texas
---
Texas took a significant hit on Mother's Day. Hail, damaging winds, and tornadoes moved through a wide corridor from the Big Country through Central Texas and into parts of South Texas. Roofs were battered across a large portion of the state.

The insurance process is now underway across that entire corridor.

A statewide severe weather event creates a specific problem that most policyholders do not anticipate: adjuster capacity. There are not enough licensed Texas adjusters to handle claims volume from a storm this large. Carriers bring in independent adjusters from other states - people working from standardized forms without familiarity with local building costs, local materials, or local code requirements.

Underpayment following a major Texas storm is not an outlier result. It is the predictable outcome when adjusters working under time pressure use national pricing databases to estimate repairs in a Texas market that consistently runs above national averages.

Homeowners who accept the first estimate and settle their claim often find later that the payment did not cover what repairs actually cost. Some of that shortfall can be addressed after the fact. Whether it can be depends on the exact language of the settlement and how much time has passed since the claim was closed.

Texas law gives carriers specific obligations in how they handle property claims. Those obligations include written timelines, written denial explanations, and itemized payment breakdowns. Most policyholders are never told those rights exist.
